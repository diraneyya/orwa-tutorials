Your vertical PVC tower in Almere is a chemistry experiment with lettuce on top. Three numbers decide whether it thrives or stalls: p H, the bicarbonate buffer in your reservoir water, and water temperature. This tutorial walks through each, explains why they behave the way they do, and shows what to actually buy at Ranzijn Tuin & Dier and Action to manage them — with a deliberate bias toward bargain solutions that match a hobby-scale test setup.

Along the way I will gloss the Dutch terms on the boxes. The aquarium aisle reads like a chemistry vocabulary lesson, and once you know that [nl: hardheid] just means hardness and [nl: voeding] just means nutrition, the labels start telling you exactly what is inside.

1. The three numbers that decide success.

Before any shopping, here is what you are trying to keep in range. Lettuce and radish are forgiving crops, but their forgiveness ends at the edges of these windows.

The three target windows. p H — 5.8 to 6.2 sweet spot, 5.5 to 6.8 acceptable. E C — 850 to 1200 microsiemens vegetative, 1200+ for radish bulbs. Reservoir temperature — 18 to 22 degrees optimum.

p H controls which nutrients your plants can absorb. E C tells you how much nutrient is in the water. Reservoir temperature controls the rate at which plants eat anything at all. The first two you can dose; the third you can only insulate, shade, or heat.

For lettuce specifically the p H sweet spot is closer to five point six to six point two; for radish in bulb phase it climbs a little to six point zero to six point eight. Both fit comfortably inside the broader window.

2. Why p H always drifts upward.

Your A plus B nutrient mix is nitrate-dominant — most of the nitrogen arrives as nitrate. When roots take up nitrate (a negative ion), they release hydroxide ( hydroxide ) to keep their internal charge balanced. Hydroxide is alkaline. So the more your plants eat, the more your reservoir p H climbs.

The drift rule. Healthy plants eating nitrate → p H goes up. The cure is acid, almost never base. If p H falls on its own → root rot, not chemistry.

Drift rate scales with how much the plant is eating. In week one, with seedlings, drift is about plus zero point one per week — check weekly. In weeks two and three, with true leaves emerging, it climbs to plus zero point two to zero point four per week — check twice weekly. From week four onward in a small reservoir, drift can hit plus zero point three to zero point five per day — check daily.

A reservoir that is drifting upward fast means happy plants. A reservoir whose p H will not budge — like a cold one at twelve degrees — means plants in slow mode. A reservoir whose p H drops on its own is sick.

3. The bicarbonate buffer — why tap water is patient.

Almere tap water comes out of the limestone aquifer underneath the city carrying about one hundred fifty to two hundred milligrams per liter of bicarbonate. This is a chemical buffer — it absorbs p H changes in both directions, like a sponge that soaks up acid or base before the p H reading actually moves.

The carbonate buffer chain. carbon dioxide + water ⇌ carbonic acid ⇌ hydrogen ion + bicarbonate. This pair sits at p H 5 to 7 — exactly your hydroponic range. Acid added → bicarbonate eats it → barely a p H change.

Practical consequences for your reservoir. Alkalinity is not p H — two waters at the same p H can have wildly different buffer capacity. Almere tap is high alkalinity. Rainwater has effectively none. The first acid drops are wasted on the buffer: they neutralize bicarbonate, and only after the buffer is partially consumed does p H actually start moving. Letting tap water sit out does not fix this — that trick works for chlorine, but bicarbonate stays put. Buffer is fully exhausted only below p H four point three, so long before then your dosing budget is fine.

This is why rainwater behaves dramatically differently in a hydroponic reservoir. Small acid additions swing p H wildly because there is nothing to absorb them. Both water sources have advantages, but they require different dosing protocols.

4.3 What K H actually measures.

K H is literally the bicarbonate concentration in your water, expressed as degrees of carbonate hardness. The Dutch term on the box is [nl: Carbonaathardheid] — exactly the same word as the German [de: Karbonathärte], just spelled with a "d" at the end instead of "te". The English word "hardness" is the same root: hard, [nl: hard], [de: hart].

The conversion: one degree of carbonate hardness equals seventeen point eight six milligrams per liter of bicarbonate.

K H benchmarks. Rainwater — 0 to 1 °dKH (no buffer, easy to overshoot). Target for hydroponics — 2 to 4 °dKH (gentle buffer). Almere tap — 8 to 11 °dKH (strong buffer). Aachen tap — 12 to 18 °dKH (very hard). Demi / R O water — 0 °dKH.

If your reservoir is on Almere tap, expect to dose plenty of acid the first time. If you switch to rainwater, expect a tiny pinch of acid powder to do the same job.

4.4 G H — total calcium and magnesium.

G H is [nl: Totale Hardheid] in Dutch, [de: Gesamthärte] in German — total hardness, measuring dissolved calcium and magnesium together. For lettuce and radish at modest E C, this is rarely a problem — your A plus B nutrient mix already provides what plants need. G H becomes useful only if you ever see calcium or magnesium deficiency symptoms, like tip burn on lettuce hearts, or if you scale up to demanding crops like tomatoes. For now, skip.

5. Adjusting p H — citric acid is the right answer.

5.1 What Ranzijn offers — and why to skip it.

The Ranzijn p H adjustment shelf has exactly one option, and it is overpriced.

This is [nl: SuperFish p H Min], one liter bottle, fourteen euros fifty.

[nl: Min] is the Dutch shorthand for minus — p H minus, the same as English "p H down". The product is most likely a dilute phosphoric or sulfuric acid solution; the back panel does not make the active ingredient obvious:

It would work, but you are paying retail for water with a small percentage of acid, you cannot see exactly what the active ingredient is, and it is pond-dosing-formulated (fifty milliliters per thousand liters of pond — far more dilute than you need for a thirty liter reservoir).

5.2 Citric acid powder from Action — the right answer.

You want citric acid powder from a supermarket or [nl: drogist] (drugstore, same root as the German [de: Drogist]). Action carries five hundred gram bags for two to four euros. Food-grade, no fumes, no toxic surprises, lifetime supply for a hobby reservoir.

Why citric acid beats commercial p H down for your scale. You know the active ingredient by name and you control the concentration. It biodegrades naturally — won't permanently load your reservoir with phosphate or sulfate. And it costs one tenth per acid-equivalent.

The one caveat is biodegradation itself: p H rebounds in a few days as microbes eat the citrate. That is actually fine for a hobby system where you check p H every few days anyway.

5.3 Stock solutions and dosing protocol.

Two stock solutions. Tap water reservoir — 5 grams citric acid in 100 milliliters water. Rainwater reservoir — 0.5 grams in 100 milliliters water (10 times more dilute).

The reason for two stocks: tap water has a huge bicarbonate buffer that absorbs acid before p H moves, so you need a strong stock. Rainwater has no buffer, so a weak stock prevents overshooting from p H seven down to p H four with one drop.

Dosing protocol. Add 1 milliliters of stock at a time. Stir, wait 5 minutes for mixing. Re-test p H with the drop kit. Repeat until in target range.

For a tap-water reservoir starting at p H seven point eight, expect the first few milliliters to do nothing. That is the buffer absorbing the first wave. Keep going in one milliliters increments. Then suddenly p H will start to fall. The transition is sharp because once the bicarbonate is locally depleted, the next drops act directly on water.

Skip white vinegar (worse rebound, smell), lemon or lime juice (sugars feed microbial slime), pool p H down with sodium bisulfate (not food grade), and battery acid (dangerous and totally inappropriate). If you ever upgrade for serious scale, food-grade phosphoric acid seventy five percent from a Dutch homebrew shop is around ten euros for one liter and lasts years — but absolutely not needed for hobby scale.

6. Nutrients — a quick word on what Ranzijn does NOT replace.

You already have your A plus B mix from Bol. Ranzijn does not stock proper hydroponic nutrients, but it stocks something that looks similar and is a trap.

This is [nl: Colombo FloraGrow Pro Special Fertilizer], five hundred milliliter bottle, nineteen euros ninety five.

The Dutch word for fertilizer is [nl: meststof] or [nl: voeding] — feed or nutrition. FloraGrow Pro looks like it should be a plant food, and it is — but it is formulated for planted aquariums, where the tank already gets nitrogen from fish waste. So it is deliberately low or zero on nitrate and ammonium. In a fish-less hydroponic tower, this would starve your plants of the main macronutrient.

The same warning applies to its smaller siblings on the same shelf — Flora Grow at two fifty milliliter, FloraGrow Carbo, Nutri Caps, FE Tabs. They are tools for diagnosing specific micronutrient deficiencies on top of a real nutrient base. Keep your Bol A plus B as the base. Do not switch.

The one scenario where one of these could be useful: if you ever see clear iron chlorosis on new leaves despite good p H, a small dose of an iron-rich one (FE Tabs) could supplement. But that is a diagnostic tool, not a daily food.

7. Water temperature — the variable doing the most damage right now.

Your tower reservoir is currently around twelve degrees Celsius because Dutch spring weather has been chilly. This is not dangerous, but it is quietly limiting everything the plants do.

7.1 The physics — two opposing effects.

Two competing temperature effects drive hydroponics:

Cold water — more dissolved oxygen. At 12 degrees — 10.8 milligrams per liter at saturation. At 22 degrees — 8.7 milligrams per liter. At 28 degrees — 7.8 milligrams per liter. Roots breathe through dissolved oxygen.

So colder water is better for root health. Root rot is essentially impossible at twelve degrees — the cold inhibits the anaerobic bacteria and the Pythium and Phytophthora fungi that drive rot.

Warm water — faster metabolism. At 12 degrees — about 35 percent of optimum uptake. At 18 degrees — about 75 percent. At 22 degrees — 100 percent optimum. At 28 degrees — 120 percent but root rot risk rises.

Plant biochemistry follows the Arrhenius rule of thumb: enzyme rates roughly double every ten degrees, until you hit the temperature where enzymes start to denature, around thirty five degrees for most plants.

7.2 What this means for your tower right now.

At twelve degrees with lettuce and radish (both cool-weather crops), you are not in danger. No root rot risk, no nutrient lockout, no enzyme damage. But growth is about a third of optimum — plants are eating slowly, p H is drifting slowly, E C is dropping slowly. Everything is in slow motion.

Disease risk is very low — bacteria and fungi slow down even more than plants do.

This is actually a fine state to learn in. You have time to read meters, adjust dosing, and observe without the system running away from you.

7.3 What you can do about it.

Warming options ranked by effort. Wait for warmer weather — free, +0. Insulate the reservoir — 5 to 15 euros, +2 to +4 degrees. Move tower to a sunny spot — free, +3 to +6 degrees. Aquarium heater 50 watt — 15 to 25 euros, +5 to +10 degrees.

For an outdoor Dutch tower in May, insulation plus sun is the cheapest fix. The fifteen euro aquarium heater is the durable answer if you plan to run the tower past autumn — small enough to leave in the bucket year-round, prevents winter freezing.

A reservoir wrapped in bubble wrap does not just raise the average temperature — it flattens the daily swing. An unwrapped Dutch outdoor reservoir in May can swing from eight degrees at dawn to twenty five degrees at three pm. That seventeen degree swing stresses roots more than a constant twelve does. Bubble wrap might only raise the average to fourteen, but it shrinks the swing to maybe twelve to eighteen — much gentler.

7.4 Quick diagnostic — is temperature your bottleneck?.

You can confirm temperature is the limit by watching how fast p H and E C drift. If p H barely moves over five days and E C barely drops, plants are barely eating — temperature is the bottleneck. If p H rises noticeably over five days and E C drops, plants are eating well — focus on p H management. If p H falls on its own, that is root rot, which is unlikely at twelve degrees but worth checking.

If p H is barely moving (the likely case right now), do not bother dosing acid. The plants are not generating hydroxide to push p H up, so there is nothing to correct. Cold reservoirs need less p H management, not more.

9. Quick start protocol for a fresh reservoir.

Two scripts for the first fill, depending on your water source.

Fresh reservoir with rainwater. 1 — Fill reservoir with rainwater. 2 — Add A and B nutrients to E C 900 to 1200. 3 — Stir, wait 10 minutes. 4 — Test p H — expect 5.8 to 6.3. 5 — Set pump duty cycle for stage. 6 — Recheck every 2 to 3 days.

Rainwater plus A plus B usually arrives in your target p H window without any acid. The hard part with rainwater is not overshooting if you do need to dose.

Fresh reservoir with tap water. 1 — Fill with Almere tap water. 2 — Add A and B to E C 900 to 1200 (deduct ~400 tap baseline). 3 — Stir, wait 10 minutes. 4 — Test p H — expect 7.4 to 7.8 (high). 5 — Dose strong citric stock 1 milliliters at a time. 6 — Continue until p H 6.0 — expect 5 to 10 milliliters total. 7 — Recheck every 3 to 5 days.

Tap-water reservoirs need acid dosing on day one and again every few days as the buffer slowly replenishes from any tap-water top-ups. The first few milliliters of acid will appear to do nothing — that is the bicarbonate buffer absorbing them. Stay patient and keep dosing in small increments.
