1. What aloe vera actually wants in its soil.

Aloe vera is a cam succulent from arid East Africa and the Arabian Peninsula.

The species' entire biology assumes brief, intense rain events followed by long dry spells. Roots flood, then air out for weeks. Native habitat is gravelly, rocky, slightly weathered volcanic soil — not the dark humic earth that [nl: potgrond] tries to simulate. Roots must have oxygen available continuously. If they sit in saturated soil, they suffocate, then opportunistic fungi (Pythium, Fusarium) move in and the plant rots from the base up.

The three things that matter in the substrate. Fast drainage. Low organic content. A mix of particle sizes.

A useful mental model: imagine the substrate as a rock pile with some soil between the rocks, not as a soil with some rocks scattered through it. That is the right ratio — and the particle sizes should include grit two to ten millimeters across.

Target p H is six point zero to seven point five — slightly acidic to neutral. Almere tap water at p H seven point eight with bicarbonate buffer drifts the substrate alkaline over time. Aloe tolerates this fine.

2. Why your sandy garden soil is "almost right" but probably not ideal.

This is the sandy ground in Almere the potting soil was dug from. Notice it is pale and fine — and that weeds and seedlings grow in it on their own.

Sandy soil from your Almere garden is closer to right than ordinary potting soil. Sand has good drainage and low organic content — both green flags for aloe. This is why the big rootstock pieces have not outright died.

But three problems likely apply. First, Dutch coastal sand is mostly fine grains around 0.1 to 0.5 millimeters, well sorted by glaciers and wind. Fine sand packs tightly when wet, then crusts when dry. Roots cannot breathe easily through this. What you want is a mix of particle sizes, including chunks two to ten millimeters across.

Second, soil from a garden bed has invisible passengers. You already noted woodlice — [nl: pissebedden] — in the pot. They are not a direct threat, but their presence means the substrate has enough organic decomposition to feed them, which means it is also feeding root-rot fungi.

Third, fine sand holds water at the bottom of the pot. Water travels downward, hits the pot floor, and capillary action makes it cling to the fine particles instead of draining. The bottom two to three centimeters of a sandy pot stays saturated even after the surface looks dry. This is exactly where rot starts.

The fact that leaves are yellow-green and drooping but the base is not blackened yet suggests the plant is in stress mode but has not crossed into active rot. That is a window of opportunity — repot before rot starts.

Compare this: another, younger aloe planted in darker soil with perlite and clay pellets mixed in. It stands more upright, and its center is green and growing. Same plant, different soil, different outcome — which is exactly what this guide is trying to show.

3. The recipe — what a real succulent mix looks like.

A proven succulent and aloe mix is roughly:

The succulent recipe. 50 to 60 percent — mineral grit (drainage, air pockets). 30 to 40 percent — low-organic substrate (moisture buffer). 0 to 10 percent — extra structure (bark, pumice, lava).

The mineral grit can be any combination of perlite, pumice, coarse horticultural grit, expanded clay pellets, or lava rock fines. Particle sizes range from one to sixteen millimeters depending on the material. All of them serve the same function: keeping air pockets between the soil particles so roots can breathe even when the medium is moist.

The most common form of expanded clay in Dutch garden stores is sold as [nl: Hydrokorrels] — clay balls baked until they puff up. The Dutch word [nl: korrels] means granules, the same root as the German [de: Körner]. So [nl: hydrokorrels] translates literally as water-granules.

The white granular alternative is perlite — volcanic glass popped like popcorn, lightweight and sterile. Ranzijn does not stock it. Praxis does.

For the substrate component you have three main options: coconut coir, peat-free [nl: potgrond], or a pre-mixed [nl: cactusgrond]. The cheapest by far — and one you already own — is a compressed cocos brick from Action.

4.1 Universal potting soils — the workhorses.

These are the cheapest, most generic bags. Brown, peat-heavy, formulated to be "good enough" for everything — which means perfect for nothing.

Peat — the dark, slow-decomposing bog material these bags are heavy with — holds water hard, which is the opposite of what aloe wants.

This is the most important wrong-for-aloe category to identify, because the bags look similar to the others and you could grab one by mistake. These are designed to LOWER soil p H for acid-loving plants.

[nl: Heide] means heather. Rhodo is rhododendron. [nl: Zuurminnend] means acid-loving — [nl: zuur] is acid, [nl: minnend] is loving. This is the peat-rich, acidified mix for that whole family. For aloe: wrong, categorically. But if you ever want to grow blueberries in a pot, or rescue a yellowing rhododendron (yellow leaves on acid-lovers usually means iron lockout from soil that is too alkaline), this bag is your tool.

4.5 Seed-starting and cutting soils.

These are fine-textured, low-nutrient mixes designed for the germination phase.

4.7 Compost — too rich for aloe pots.

This is [nl: Ranzijn Compost Voedingsrijk], thirty liter bag, six euros twenty five (three for sixteen).

[nl: Voedingsrijk] means nutrient-rich: [nl: voeding] is food or nutrition, [nl: rijk] is rich. Aged organic matter, dark, crumbly, nutrient-dense.

For aloe: skip. Compost belongs in vegetable beds and at the planting hole of new trees and shrubs. In a pot — especially for a desert plant — it is too rich and decomposes too actively, releasing carbon dioxide in the root zone and displacing oxygen.

4.8 Drainage components — the most useful category for aloe.

This is where you find what aloe actually needs. We already met [nl: Hydrokorrels] in section three; now to put them in context.

This is [nl: Florentus Hydrokorrels] again, ten liter for six euros ninety nine — also known internationally as LECA, Lightweight Expanded Clay Aggregate.

Baked clay balls, four to sixteen millimeters in size. The bag label says "verbetert wateropname en voorkomt uitdroging" — improves water uptake and prevents drying out. How it works: the clay balls are full of tiny pores that hold a thin film of water without being saturated. They never compact, never break down, and let air flow freely between them. Indefinite lifespan.

For aloe: essential. Use as a drainage layer at the bottom of the pot (the bottom two to three centimeters), or mix into the substrate at about twenty five to thirty percent by volume to create air pockets throughout.

Ranzijn also stocks bigger volumes — [nl: Florentus Hydrokorrels] at forty liter for sixteen euros ninety five if you are scaling up, and [nl: Pokon Hydrokorrels] at twenty liter for eleven euros ninety five (same product, different brand).

These do not go inside the pot; they go on top. They serve two purposes: hold moisture in the soil by reducing surface evaporation, and look tidy.

4.10 Lime — for raising soil p H.

This is [nl: Green Boots Tuinkalk], four kilogram box, three euros ninety nine.

[nl: Tuinkalk] is garden lime — pulverized calcium carbonate — same root word in Dutch and German ([de: Gartenkalk]). Spread on the soil to raise p H (make less acidic, more alkaline).

For aloe: not needed. Almere tap water is already alkaline, your garden soil here is likely slightly alkaline already, and aloe tolerates the full range from six point zero to seven point five. Adding lime would push p H higher than necessary.

For what then? Lawns where moss is taking over (moss prefers acidic conditions; raising p H discourages it). Vegetable beds where soil has become acidic over years of nitrogen fertilizer use. Around stone fruit trees. Brassica beds — they are sensitive to club root, a fungal disease worse in acidic soil.

5. The compromise recipe — using only Ranzijn products.

If you do not want to make a second trip and just want to improve on your current sandy soil, here is the best you can do at Ranzijn:

The Ranzijn-only recipe. Bottom 3 centimeters — Florentus Hydrokorrels (drainage layer). 2 parts — Florentus Mediterrane Potgrond. 1 part — Florentus Hydrokorrels (mixed in). 1 part — sterilized sandy garden soil.

To sterilize the garden soil, bake it in a tray at eighty to one hundred degrees Celsius for thirty minutes, or pour boiling water through it. This kills weed seeds, pathogens, [nl: pissebedden] and their eggs. Cool fully before mixing.

This gets you to roughly forty percent mineral and fifty percent medium, with ten percent sand. Not the textbook fifty fifty, but a real improvement over pure sandy garden soil. 6. The proper recipe — and what is missing at Ranzijn.

If you are willing to make one more stop, the proper succulent mix is much better. The recipe:

The proper succulent recipe. 1 part — coconut coir or peat-free potgrond (substrate). 1 part — perlite (light drainage, air). 1 part — coarse grit or pumice (heavy drainage, structure).

Good news: you already own the substrate component — the [nl: MyGarden Compressed Cocos] brick from Action expands to twenty five liters when soaked, plenty for all three aloe pots with leftover.

You just need the mineral grit components — perlite plus pumice or grit. Ranzijn does not stock these. Praxis does, and there is also a cheaper specialty option (pre-mixed [nl: cactusgrond]) at Action.

6.1 Cactusgrond / Vetplantengrond — the pre-mixed shortcut.

This is Pokon Cactus Potgrond, five liter bag at Praxis for about six to eight euros.

[nl: Vetplant] means succulent — literally "fat plant", same idea as the German [de: Fettpflanze]. The bag is small because you do not need much — five liters fills one or two aloe pots.

This is the same product family as the other Pokon [nl: potgronds], but blended with extra perlite and grit so the texture is gritty rather than fluffy. Out of the bag, this is the closest thing to a "just open and pour" succulent mix. Similar products to look for: [nl: Florentus Cactus en Vetplanten Potgrond], [nl: Culvita Cactusgrond], [nl: Intratuin Cactusgrond RHP].

The cheapest option in this category is at Action stores — they often stock a generic [nl: Cactus en Vetplantengrond] six liter bag for one euro ninety nine to two ninety nine. Same idea, lower price, slightly less consistent quality. For aloe rescue at home scale, perfectly fine.

6.2 Perlite — perliet in Dutch.

This is DCM Perliet, ten liter bag at Praxis for around ten to thirteen euros.

Volcanic glass that has been heated until it pops like popcorn — turns into lightweight white granules, two to six millimeters in size, full of tiny pores. p H-neutral, sterile (no fungi, no weed seeds), holds a tiny bit of water in its pores, lets air flow freely. Does not compress. Lasts indefinitely.

In a succulent mix, perlite is the workhorse aerator. Mix one part perlite into two parts of any potting soil and you have already significantly improved drainage. Beyond succulents, perlite is used for starting seeds (sterile medium prevents damping-off disease), rooting cuttings, hydroponics, and as a soil amendment for heavy clay garden beds.

6.3 Pumice — bims in Dutch — and coarse grit.

For a pre-blended bonsai-grade mineral substrate, Praxis carries a Pokon Bonsai option that includes pumice, perlite, and lava rock.

This is Pokon Bonsai Potgrond, five liter bag at Praxis for about seven euros.

Pumice — [nl: bims] in Dutch, [de: Bims] in German (same word, same volcanic stone) — is heavier than perlite, similarly porous, also in the three to eight millimeter range. Does not crush over time, which makes it preferred over perlite for long-lived collections (cacti, bonsai). Sold standalone in Dutch bonsai shops, typically fifteen to twenty five euros for a five liter bag.

Coarse horticultural grit is angular silica or basalt rock fragments, one to four millimeters. Mostly drainage, very little water retention. The cheapest mineral component if you can find it — try Welkoop or the Praxis aquarium section. Aquarium gravel works fine if it is labeled calcium-free, [nl: kalkvrij] in Dutch.

For bonsai enthusiasts: the classic substrate trio is akadama (fired Japanese clay), pumice, and lava rock fines. These are all valid in a succulent mix too — overkill in cost for a single aloe, but useful to know if you ever build a serious collection.

7. Repotting protocol for your big aloe pieces.

Given the leaves are stressed but the base is not rotted, repot soon — within the next week. Process:

Wait for soil to be bone dry. Do not water for at least three to five days before repotting. It is easier to remove a dry rootball without breaking roots.. Gently lift the plant out of the current pot. Tip the pot sideways, tap the rim, slide the rootball out. If it resists, run a knife around the inside edge of the pot.. Inspect the roots and base. White or cream firm roots are healthy — keep them. Brown, mushy or smelly tissue — cut back with a clean knife until you reach firm tissue, then let air-dry for twenty four hours before potting. Black or soft at the base means root rot is advanced — cut all rotten tissue away, dust with cinnamon (a mild natural antifungal) or charcoal powder, and restart phase one callusing.. Shake off the old sandy soil. Do not scrub the roots — just remove what falls off naturally.. Prepare the new pot. Use a terracotta pot if possible (porous, lets soil dry between waterings — much better for aloe than plastic). Drainage hole essential. Size: only slightly larger than the rootball — aloe likes to be a bit root-bound.. Layer the substrate. Bottom two to three centimeters: [nl: hydrokorrels] (drainage layer). Above that: your succulent mix.. Position the plant so the base is at the same level it was before — no deeper, since burying the stem causes rot.. Fill in around the rootball, tap the pot to settle, top with a one centimeter layer of coarse grit or hydrokorrels. Top-dressing prevents soil splash, looks tidy, and deters fungus gnats.. Do NOT water for five to seven days. This lets any micro-damage to roots heal. Watering immediately after repotting is one of the most common ways to kill a recovering plant. The plant has plenty of water stored in its leaves..

8. Watering protocol after repotting.

Once watering resumes, the frequency rule is: soil bone dry to about five centimeters depth, and leaves slightly wrinkled. Then water. Otherwise wait.

For the method, soak the soil thoroughly until water runs out of the drainage hole. Then empty any saucer underneath — never let the pot sit in standing water.

Frequency in practice: spring and summer outdoors, roughly every ten to fourteen days. Autumn and winter, every three to four weeks. Less is always safer than more.

Always water at the side of the pot, not on the stem or center of the plant. This keeps the crown dry and prevents rot from above.

Underwatering an aloe gives wrinkled leaves, which recover within hours after water. Overwatering gives a blackened base, which is fatal. The asymmetry is large — err dry.
